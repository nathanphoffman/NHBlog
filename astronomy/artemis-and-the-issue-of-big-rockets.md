# Artemis and the issue of big rockets

Posted on Medium: 2026-04-20

![](/images/0-9UMacE9SlTfXTZBN.jpg)

I watched Artemis II along with many people earlier this month, infact Buzz Aldrin himself apparently watched it and it got his 96 year old-self excited.

It got me thinking about how a younger me would be taking a very different stance: SLS (the rocket system for Artemis) was a bad compromise and a waste of money, just give it to commercial companies. It is a sentiment I have seen the same phrase repeated online, yet is rarely uttered by those who started in space (like 3 of the 4 living Astronauts who walked on the moon who have made comments on how much they love Artemis).

I have also heard “Apollo did this 50+ years ago, what the hell guys all you can do is go around the Moon?” These are all rooted in very real frustrations and problems, but they are forgetting a few important facts: rocketry is hard, rocketry is not always intuitive, and safety and budgets are of a different scale than they were during Apollo (when adjusted by inflation).

The Saturn V (the rocket that launched the most historic Apollo missions like the first to go around the moon and the first to land on it) was capable of launching approximately 48 Metric Tons to TLI (Trans Lunar Injection to the Moon). This was useful since the lander, capsule, service module, crew, fuel, and cargo all weighed about that much. It also wasn’t free: the Apollo program cost well over 300 billion dollars adjusted for 2026 inflation. On the otherhand the SLS Block 1 only has a 27 Metric Ton capacity, and despite wasting huge amounts of money in delays and serving as a jobs program more for preserving Space Shuttle jobs than launching people to the Moon, they still have yet to even spend 1/3 as much as Apollo (though I should mention they have only flown 2 test missions).

Some have asked why didn’t we just do Apollo again why this smaller mission?! But they miss the point, to satisfy the general public, commercial profits, and modern human safety standards:
- **Any trip back to the Moon needs to be better than Apollo**, any mission that is the same or lesser in anyway will be highly ridiculed. Infact even though Artemis II is doing better things (4 astronauts, toilet, food warmers, better power systems and solar arrays, 60% increase in physical space in the capsule — even more per person) they aren’t good enough for most people since they didn’t land on the Moon— so infact it needs to be substantially better and more impressive. And of course substantially better and bigger means **a lot** more weight, and a larger rocket.
- **It needs to be done within the tenuous political and financial landscape.** This landscape changes with each administration and the changing of congress. Sometimes, even when budgets are increased, the budgets are still be moved around in such a way that it makes the program hard to run.
- **Modern rockets (esp. crewed rockets) need to be 2 stages** with possible boosters if tested (3 or more presents logistical and safety risks). While 2 stages are perfect for LEO (low earth orbit), the physics provide very little margin for getting out to the Moon and make it so the rocket needs to be even bigger than the Saturn V, thus undoing some of the savings and adding more costs. Apollo was more concerned with getting to the Moon by the end of the decade than with safety, that recklessness can be seen in the 3 astronauts who lost their lives on a test pad, a fact most seem to forget.
- **We really should reuse the 1st stage.** While technically not really a requirement, and reusing the second stage is rarely worth it: reusing the 1st makes so much sense we probably should, unfortunately it does lower cargo capacity a bit, and companies like SpaceX are intent on reusing the second stage despite it massively reducing capacity when you are trying to fling something a quarter million miles away.
- **We have different safety standards now** that means more testing, more auditing, higher standards (1/270 is the current human loss minimum-standard set by NASA, though they conveniently, and I would say recklessly, lowered it to 1/30 for Artemis). This overhead also adds time, and time is money as the old addage goes. It also adds weight if additional radiation shielding and supplied are needed (and thus bigger rocket requirements).

But this is not even close to the biggest problem. The problem lies in what these problems create, and further an issue those problems create: an issue that has plagued modern rocketry: if you are going to reuse a rocket, add weight for safety and for improvements, and simultaneously limit its capability for testability by limiting it to two stages how the heck do you expect to do it with something even as big as a Saturn V: heck even a bit bigger?

You (probably) can’t. At least not with one rocket.

![](/images/0-hNyxyDpN2JO_V8fb.jpg)

The problem we face is that rockets tend to work best when they can afford to lose an engine (or two) and when they don’t have so many engines that the risk of a catastrophic failure becomes too high, it tends to be something like 4–12 engines, the number isn’t exactly set some will say 5 is the minimum, others might extend the upper bound close to 20 or so, but for sure ~30ish seems to be beyond if not what is possible, at least beyond what is reliable. This is about the number of engines that Elon Musk is using in Starship (quite often unsuccessfully) and it is also the number of engines used in the N-1 which infamously failed all of its 4 flights. The problem is that with increasing engines, you also increase the engine failure rate and if the failure is catastrophic, then you lose the whole rocket (and possibly crew).

A logical conclusion is that we should make the engines bigger, but larger engines are less stable, this is well proven in rocketry: voids and instability are more likely to form within the engine cavity which can translate to a cascading catastrophic failure. It was also seen in the F1 (the engine that drove the Apollo program) which had extreme instability issues that it cost a small fortune and army of engineers to fix, and even when corrected no one was really sure why the corrections worked (a risk we almost certainly would not take today). While instability can be minimized by adding more nozzles, doing so adds even more complexity as each engine effectively becomes a multi-subengine contraption (which is probably why very few were ever made like the Energia engine).

So interestingly (but also unfortunately) since the number of engines are limited, and their size is limited, it gives us a bit of a maximum upper bound for how large of a rocket we can even fly. It likely lies somewhere in the neighborhood of a Starship or even slightly smaller given histories proof that rockets of this scale tend to be extraordinarily difficult to develop.

So now we circle back. We are limited to the rocket size, and yet we are faced with (perhaps) a 60% reduction in budget, a 30% reduction in first stage re-usability, increasing safety standards and testing costs for any rocket that would even come close to solving these issues. So we come to the conclusion that the SLS engineers came to 10 years ago: you need more than one. That is the only reliable way to send large human missions into deep space. But that also means potentially more cost, and an even higher desire for reliability since you need multiple rockets which yet feeds this whole feedback loop of costs and delays.

But multiple rockets cause yet another problem: how do you dock all these parts in orbit? Worse, since fuel makes the most sense to split out into tankers as it is the most heavy part of missions, how do you refuel? These fuels tend to be very sensitive, O2 needed for nearly all fuels and most liquid fuels boil off slowly releasing into space and decreasing which means the refueling is also time-limited. And worse yet, we never have demonstrated refueling at all in space, let alone pressurized liquid refueling which is much harder than you might think as liquid fuels don’t sink to the bottom of a tank, infact we have barely figured out how to put a gas gauge on these tanks (since the fuel literally floats around).

The original SLS plan (which was to use 2 launches) mostly solves all of these issues, but unfortunately it doesn’t really solve everything: its not reusable, and it’s not really that much more impressive than Apollo. You do the SLS Block 1 Crew (like the Artemis II mission going on as I post this) and you pair it with a SLS Block 2 Cargo (an even bigger and more risky rocket) for a lander with more fuel to help take the crew into lunar orbit. If the lander fails to reach the crew on their return (or the bigger more risky rocket blows up) the crew is already on their way home (like Artemis II did).

And the ironic thing is, I was one of those angry internet people back in the day “just give SpaceX the money” but from where I sit, I honestly think if you want to get back to the moon in the next decade the SLS Block 2 Cargo is the only way to do it. Yea its not so impressive, but it doesn’t require tech we don’t have (in space refueling) it doesn’t require space stations (yes SpaceX wants to build a space station first), it doesn’t require 6-20 (depending on who you believe) refueling missions (like Starship) to even perform a minimum viable mission to the moon, it doesn’t require a rocket not yet even close to complete and again (the aforementioned refueling) like Bezos plans.

Yet, people (including politicians) will be quick to point out “its too similar to Apollo, and it costs too much” but then they turn around and say “why haven’t we gone to the moon must have been faked folks!” The truth is… Apollo was just about as perfect a moon landing craft as one could make, likely beyond the costs congress wants to spend, and it’s on the upper end of what is even remotely “safe” in terms of size, and very likely beyond the risks we would take today. Unfortunately, it was not grandiose enough, or reusable enough to meet any bar anyone sets before it today even if we did produce it for less than its 300 billion original budget. And of course those same folks would still say it was too expensive.

At the end of the day, you can’t change physics, material science, or chemistry. Computers don’t run rockets: fuel, engines and materials do; computers merely help in design and optimization. Physics, chemistry, and material have not changed since the 60s and engines are basically the same.

So where do we go from here? Well, I for one do not want to kill astronauts, and reusing the 1st stage of rockets is much more logical than upper stages, so limiting it to 2 in the name of both (and choosing whether or not to reuse the 2nd) makes sense. I also am pragmatic though, will we land on the Moon before 2030 as Artemis suggests? No, absolutely 100% not. If I had a million dollars, I would certainly bet on it not happening. Infact, I think these problems are so large that I would be shocked if we landed on the moon even before 2040. The most likely fate of Artemis program is maybe a few more test missions similar to this one, with likely paired back objectives, and all of which delayed into the 2030s (possibly even with some experimental lander in low earth orbit). Then long before we get to the Moon it will get cancelled after we realize we simply can’t do a more ambitious mission than Apollo within any kind of time frame. Just remember, its not that we can’t do Apollo again its that it simply doesn’t make sense in this day and age (for so many reasons we discussed) to do so.
