# What I learned in attempting to develop my first RPG System

**If you don’t want to read this whole thing, this is my conclusion:** D&D 5e and other systems of its ilk are so well tested, supported, and accepted that attempting to create my own system was effectively dead before I even began. The best I was able to do was try and use different systems, some more unique than others (action systems, using custom dice, changing up skills, or using 2D6 for everything from damage to to-hit to skill-checks) in an attempt to differentiate my system from D&D and others, but while such an attempt can be unique, it is not necessarily better. D&D may have its faults, but its flexibility of using polyhedral dice that anyone can buy, pencils, and character sheets (and optionally miniatures and maps) allows for such insane variability, adaptability, house-rule alteration, and pure imagination that no other system that attempts to be more restrictive or custom can ever match its scale, supporting materials, customization, or appeal to the general public.

Ultimately, it is likely better to modify an existing system like D&D (or Cypher, Pathfinder, etc.) then it is to attempt to create a new system from the bottom up. It is akin to starting your first restaurant by becoming an architect to build the restaurant building and then proceeding to attempt to run it. It would be far better to simply buy an existing restaurant, particularly one already in a good location that people know well, and make the changes and modifications you want to adapt it to your own purposes. Of course this precludes marketing and selling the system, but it does not preclude selling your ideas or house-rules.

Some ways I might personally modify DnD are: **removing the proficiency bonus system** (I could slightly buff modifiers to counter and would instead subtract from a player’s roll if they weren’t proficient with a tool they were using), **simplifying modifiers** by making them checkboxes with a modifier attached to the box (maybe -1 default, +1, +3, +5) and just telling players “You can check X number of checkboxes” at creation & at certain levels, **rolling less** which the DM can rule simply by allowing say anything of DC12 or less to succeed no matter what, **making helper sheets** so that players can reference what abilities they have unlocked so far and have a single sheet to reference for what they can do, and **using cards for items** to prevent as much writing down and make the players feel like they have actual items infront of them.
