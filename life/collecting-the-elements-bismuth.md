I started collecting samples of elements just a few weeks ago. My main inspiration was the YouTube channel [Periodic Videos](https://www.youtube.com/user/periodicvideos) where they talk about each and every known element. I figured I would document things as I ordered and received samples of each element. (Obviously there will be a few I will not be able to obtain)

**Bismuth**
- Bismuth forms brilliant colorful-square crystalline structures when rapidly cooled. As a result, man-made bismuth crystal is fairly common and can be found in souvenir shops, mineral shops, science shops, etc. It was actually as a souvenir that I purchased the sample in the vial when I was quite young. (pictured above)
- The isotope of Bismuth that is most commonly found is 209. This isotope is generally regarded as the last safe and non-radioactive element on the periodic table. (see point below)
- Interestingly, Bismuth 209 has a virtually immeasurable amount of radioactivity with a half-life of 1.9*10¹⁹. This is over a billion times the age of the universe so it is not really considered radioactive on a human-lifespan.
- Due to its high periodic number, bismuth is a bit denser than most metals which gives it a nice weight. (denser than iron though not as dense as elements like lead)
- It is a brilliant silver when polished and non-oxidized.
- It is relatively easy to work with, flake, and shape, and can be melted at around 520.7 F, 271.5 F this makes it possible to melt on your stove-top if you can get it hot enough (I am not suggesting you do this)

**Summary:** It is hard to find a neater metal than one which is nice to look at, nice to work with, feels nice, and will not harm you. [Periodic Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vyIo-c7VmIM).
