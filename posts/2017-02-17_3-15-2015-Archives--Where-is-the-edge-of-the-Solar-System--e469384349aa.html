3/15/2015 Archives: Where is the edge of the Solar System?

Several months back, many news sites exclaimed that the Voyager spacecraft had left the Solar System. Then, a month or so ago, it was announced that a star grazed our solar system 70,000 years ago. The problem with these statements is that they suggest the Voyager spacecraft (a man made spacecraft launched in the late 70s) was as far away as a star once was from our own Sun. To any reader, it would make sense that their distances from the sun were the same since they were both at the edge of the solar system. Unfortunately, this is wrong by a factor of about 1,000x.

The problem with defining boundaries within our Solar System is that no physical boundary or border actually exists in space, and therefore we must make up our own definition for what constitutes the entirety of a Solar System. The issue is analogous to calling a friend and telling them “I am still at the mall.” This could mean, you are in the mall, outside of the mall, or even on the road very close to the mall. But what defines very close? At what point do you cease “being at the mall?” Joe might think he has left the mall when he left the building but Fred might think he has left the mall when he has left the parking lot, who is right? In the same sense, at what point does a spacecraft or object cease being in our Solar System? And which Scientist is right?

There are two common ways in which the edge of our solar system is defined:

1) The way in which many scientists talk about the “edge of our” Solar System is where the solar wind of our own Sun is balanced by the Solar wind from other stars. The problem is, the general public would not accept this definition as this does not take into account the actual matter within the solar system or the Sun’s gravity. In essence, it doesn’t account for the contents of the system.

2) The edge of the Solar System may also be defined as the boundary at which all material bound to the sun stops. This is likely the Oort Cloud (icy debris) at about 1 light year (63,000 AU) from our Sun.

Both of these definitions are fine, but a serious problem arises when they are interchanged. When Voyager had “left the solar system”, it was announced to be around 120AU from our Sun. (Method #1) But when it was announced there was a solar system grazing star, it passed within 120,000 AU from our Sun. Not quite twice the distance of method #2 but off by a factor of 1000x from Voyager’s distance (method #1).

While Pluto and most of the solar system objects are well within this boundary (actually within about 40AU) the issue is that a few planetoids (perhaps up to 100 or more) like Sedna and many Comets are often well outside this boundary of 120AU. Furthermore, small dusty material and comet fragments may lie as far as the Oort Cloud, an estimated 1 light year from the Sun. This distance is a staggering 63,000 AU. So if we truly are talking about the Solar System as the Sun’s gravitational influence on its material, then method #1 is off by an error of around 99.9%.

So when you read something is “at the edge at” or “has left” the Solar System. Make sure you focus more on the actual distance and less the hype.

NOTE: 1 light year is often quoted as the size of the Oort Cloud but I sources say it might extend to 100,000 AU. If so, this makes the difference in scale between methods 1 and 2 even larger.
