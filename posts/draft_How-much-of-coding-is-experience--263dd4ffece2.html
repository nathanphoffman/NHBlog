How much of coding is experience?

Something I always wanted to do was improve myself, to constantly reach for the stars, but in the process of doing so I have also discovered conflicting views on best practices, some folks going back to their roots “don’t fix what aint broke” and I myself have gone from loving ultra short clever array manipulations to sometimes just wanting things spelled out for simplicity. I remember at one of my jobs a fellow senior developer telling me not to use declarative function syntax in JavaScript because “that is not the new way of doing it” he was referring to named const-lambda functions vs declarative functions. He did not provide me with any real justification such as hoisting vs non-hoisting. In truth, even the debate is a bit silly as either works in 99% of cases. His approach was that different/newer is better.

When we teach people how to program we usually start simple, and the simple things we teach people is often the best way to program. Sure, we don’t get into the details of Pointers, Stack vs Heap, Enums, Array manipulations like reducing, Filters (), Symbols, Linq, Constants, or perhaps complex conditional types in Typescript, but that doesn’t change that what they are doing generally is understandable and quite frankly, fairly decent.

Something I mentioned in a prior post, and I continue to be obsessed with, is something called Miller’s Number. It is the idea that the human brain can only hold onto 5–9 things comfortably at a time. We like to act like a senior+ programmer can scan 100s of lines of code and just instantly know what is going on, but infact, they can’t (unless they already worked heavily in that code and committed it to long term memory). But just like a jr. dev they can scan the 6 functions associated with those 100s of lines or 6 file names and make a good guess as to what it does so long as it was written simply and declaratively. 

It is true that the more senior the programmer, the more frameworks they have likely used and the more language features they have come across. The problem is, particularly in the web world, things move so fast that even the most experienced of programmers can’t maintain perfect knowledge in every framework and every technique simultaneously so if one finds a Jr. Dev who has only played with something like say Mithril they are probably better off than a Senior Dev who has never seen it, at least at first.

I thought about writing this blog post after reading differing opinions on Clean Code vs Pragmatic Code and how intense both sides of the discussion were. Just given the sheer number of devs on both sides of the argument seem to indicate that neither is really the correct solution, and yet both have very different approaches to programming, so how can both be right or the “more skilled” way of doing something?

I think programming comes down to really just 8 things:

  • Your Toolkit (starting to matter less with AI)
  • Your Instinct / Wisdom(the hardest thing to replace with AI)
  • Keeping it Simple (and well named)
  • Keeping it Short if Possible (and generally, though maybe not always avoiding duplication)
  • Organization of Code (both easy and hard from an AI standpoint)
  • Weighing Performance
  • Weighing Developer Time and Resources
  • People Skills

The problem is none of this is clear cut, much of it is subjective, and not all of it is an a strength of being a more senior dev. Arguably wisdom based metrics like weighing time and performance is probably better served by someone with seniority, but oddly, some of these senior devs struggle even more than junior devs with some items like keeping things simple.

At the end of the day I think we need to acknowledge there is a blurred line between seniority. What is seniority is usually perception and vibes more than it is actual truth, even if there are some things that seniority can truly help with (like wisdom and experience).
