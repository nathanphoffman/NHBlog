Ranking System Part III

It is poetic then I come back full circle to this blog with some thoughts on a program I have made that scrapes the biggest board game site (Board Game Geek) in an effort to make a better list of board games. BGG’s (Board Game Geek’s) own ranking can be found here.

I have a fundamental issue with their rankings, notably:

  • The heaviest board games float to the top, likely because a heavy gamer will play and rank a light game — and probably hate it, while a light gamer will never play a heavy game — and thus not rate it poorly as they likely would have.
  • The newest board games are biased against — This is part of how weighting works, where fewer reviews mean that a rating is less trustworthy, but it is also problematic in board gaming where games can become dated, and those people from 15 years ago aren’t updating their reviews to reflect the times.
  • Popular games are biased-against if the review is too low. I feel like this is unfair, if a game has a 6.5/10 rating but the whole world has reviewed it, I feel like it should still be on the top 100 for its popularity alone. This might seem contradictory to my earlier statement about games with lower ratings being too highly ranked, but for me there is a bit of a curve to be had — where extremely popular games with slightly lower (though not low) ratings should be higher.
  • They use standard deviation or something similar it appears BGG uses something like standard deviation in their ranking as a game like Gloomhaven by traditional weighting should be higher in the ranking if you look at its number reviews and raw score, however, Gloomhaven has a higher concentration of 10s than something like Brass Birmingham which is likely why BGG is punishing its ranking (to counter over-hype). This may be done with good intentions, but I think countering unusually high ratings defeats the whole purpose of having a ranking. If something is super loved and heavily played and still rises past the haters, why punish it?
