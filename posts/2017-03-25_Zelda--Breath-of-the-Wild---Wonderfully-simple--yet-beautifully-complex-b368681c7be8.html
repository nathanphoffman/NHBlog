Zelda: Breath of the Wild — Wonderfully simple, yet beautifully complex

Synopsis: 10/10 | Wonderfully simple yet beautifully complex. — Me

(No major spoilers, the only spoilers here are discoverable in the first few hours of gameplay)

I have been playing Breath of the Wild more than any game I have played recently. In a short couple of weeks I am already up to about 50–60 hours of gameplay time, taking my time to explore the world, while picking at the main story here and there. At this point, I can say this is one of the best games I have ever played, and no it is not because I am on the hype train. If I didn’t have nostalgia for Metroid Prime this might even take the cake as my favorite game of all time.

Rather than go into a complicated and wordy review, let us get to the point. Why is this game so good?

  1. The Open World 
    I have not played any of the other Zelda games, but I have played many MMOs and the Elder Scrolls games, and while I may be a bit biased for open worlds, Zelda does it better than anything else I have played before. The world is gigantic, there are virtually no loading screens, even when entering buildings. The areas seamlessly transition from grassy plains, to dense forests, to snowy capped peaks and mountains to even fiery mountains/volcanoes. What makes the open world even stronger is also the modes of transportation and all of the fun secrets waiting for you, but those deserve praise all their own (see below).
  2. The Hidden Treasures
    A game with an open world needs to be more than a barrier by which you proceed from objective A to B. It needs to have a reason to explore all by itself. Zelda does this not just well, but better than so many games I have played. The MMO Elder Scrolls Online tried to make exploration a fun thing by hiding shards you could collect, but overall it felt like a gimic. This game, however, hides treasures with powerful items, shrines which are glorified puzzles that will make you stronger, Korok Seeds which simply say “if you collect enough of these something might happen, you never know”, more diverse wildlife and plants which you can use for elixirs and cooking. There are also towers which you can use to download more of the map and use as launching points to explore yet more areas. If this were not enough you can also find rare rocks/ores, large boss creatures and random travelers who might share items or side quests with you. There is even a mechanic which will require you to find specific scenes in the environment, but I will not spoil that here. Exploration isn’t just fun and productive in this game, it is mandatory.
  3. The Modes of Transportation
    Many open world games are about walking slowly and jogging at a moderate pace when you have enough stamina to do so. When you reach a sheer cliff or mountain, you have to turn around and go the other way. Zelda completely breaks these conceptions. First, the walk speed is already much faster than most games and the run is more like a sprint, allowing you to cover more of the world in more time, which is good considering how large it truly is. The game also introduces climbing, a glider and “shield surfing” all of which are epic fun. Gliding in particular I have become addicted to. I purposely climb to the highest peak of some remote mountain top just so I can jump off and glide across some large valley and swoop down on some distant mountain top. Shield surfing is also useful and somewhat hilarious as I jump on top of a shield and slide down a grassy hill for several hundred meters. Oh, and of course you can ride a horse, even strange sand beasts, and i have even seen screenshots of Link riding a bear? What is best is it isn’t like most games where you pay 6 billion gold for a horse and have to be level 50 to buy one, instead, you can find horses in the environment, sneak up on them, and ride them across large expanses.
  4. The Shrines & Puzzles 
    I mentioned “shrines that can make you stronger”. In truth these are locations which, when discovered, allow you to fast travel in the environment, but when entered and solved grant you an orb which can later be turned into health or stamina. To solve these shrines you will be required to complete a test, like a test of combat, a puzzle, or perhaps no test at all! But most require a puzzle to be solved. Many of these puzzles remind me of a Talos Principle or Portal. Not too many of them are crazy mind wrenchers, but a few have required me to Google what to do. All in all even the easier puzzles are still fun to solve, and additional treasure can be found by thinking even more out of the box. A few treasures I have just given up entirely on because they are the “hard mode” version of the puzzle and I just grab my orb and leave. There are also A TON of these shrines. Supposedly there are 120. So far I have discovered about 60 and solved around 55, it is mind blowing to me that I am not even half-way there.
  5. The Cooking
    The games major crafting component seems to be cooking and making elixirs with material you find in the environment. Again, this further makes exploration more valuable as the materials you find for cooking are typically better and more productive (like large animals) well outside of towns and cities. The cooking mechanic doesn’t even have unlockable recipe information. Instead, you have to figure it out entirely on your own. You can read books and journals people leave near camp fires or talk to people directly, having to recall on your own memory to cook those items. Otherwise you either just guess or Google. One of the nice things I have noticed about cooking/making elixirs, is that many of the recipes have patterns to them. For example if a mushroom and a meat produce a certain product, chances are any type of mushroom and any type of meat will make a product, even if they differ in result. One of the more fun, though frustrating things in the game is seeing the reaction of Link after he cooks something that does not align to a recipe. The game, rather than destroying the item, makes a mockery of it and adds “Dubious Food” into your inventory giving you a fraction of health restoration of the final product. Eating these items even has its own animation where Link tries to stomach obviously awful food.
  6. The Witty Dialog and Character/Creature Interaction
    While this game does not have a deep story/lore background like an Elder Scrolls or Mass Effect game, the fun dialog and witty humor is so refreshing for a modern game. It is surprising just how deep the conversation logic can be even though the dialog itself is very light. As an example, you might make a pun in a conversation, and the other character makes a pun right back, mocking you. Or you might not be able to talk to a character at all unless you are wearing specific clothing or have certain items equipped. Dialog options may not show, unless you had accomplished something for that specific town. Some girls in the environment will even take a liking to Link, one was even hiding from me in a shop in the corner without any good reason why she was hiding. Hell even the animals have interaction, I can feed dogs meat I just hunted from wild game, and from what I understand “something good might happen” if I keep helping the dogs out in the world. Not to mention the chickens…if you are a past Zelda player I hear you might know already about chickens…
  7. Special Powers and Abilities
    There are two types of abilities, those I will explain here as you unlock them within the first few hours of gameplay, and those I will not explain or mention at all. The ones I will not explain are surprises and you will find them on your travels, and some of them are bloody awesome. The basic ones you will get early on, these include the ability to move metal objects with a magnet, the ability to freeze a column of water, the ability to throw bombs and remote detonate them, as well as an ability to freeze time on an object. All of these become mandatory to solving later puzzles and are the “portal gun” equivalent for the game. You may even need them from time to time to solve puzzles outside of the shrines to get to items and places in the world. Like many good puzzle games, these items synergize well together and allow interesting stacking opportunities.
  8. The Diverse yet Simple Combat
    The combat is similar to the character interactions. Simple on the surface but incredibly complex should you choose to dig into all your options. You can just hack them with a sword and bow, or you can equip special arrows to cater to their weaknesses, use items you cooked to boost your stats, or even go crazy and set fires to cause updrafts to pounce on enemies with the glider, or throw a sword to an enemy in a lightning storm so he picks it up and gets struck by lightning. Seriously, this is all possible and so much more. You can also use any of those abilities we discussed before allowing you to throw metal items at enemies as well or push enemies off of a water fall with your ice column. The combat options are amazingly diverse while still keeping things simple without dozens of RPG stats.
  9. Item Breaking and Resourcefulness
    This is often quoted as a reason people dislike the game, but I absolutely love this concept. Items can break after heavy use, and it forces you to use weaker items and get creative even when you are strong, causing you to think before whipping out that super powerful item in the game! I have a sword I have yet to use because it is twice as powerful as even the master sword. I can think of no other RPG where I can be so far into a game and am forced to be creative with weaker items while I have items that are many times more powerful which I have never used.
  10. The Art and Beauty
    Many people also rate the “graphics” as a negative, but seriously how? There are times I play this game that I think the environment in front of me looks like scenes out of Bob Ross’s paintings. With epic distant mountains rising behind large forests and plains with wildlife scattered everywhere. This game is a freaking work of art, every piece of terrain so jam packed with detail it is sometimes hard for me to believe it is running on a small tablet. Yes, I know from a purely polygonal and texture perspective this game may not hold up to other consoles and certainly not modern video cards, but they did so well with the style that it feels right, looks good, and holy crap did I say it runs on a tablet?!
  11. The Atmosphere
    The ambiance of this game is amazing. The way the game feels and acts in response to weather is extremely realistic. Rain will make surfaces slick so you will be unable to climb and it will put fires out. Snow and cold environments will require you to bundle up, while desserts are impossibly hot by day but quite cool at night. Even the time of day can result in the activity of monsters and the solving of certain puzzles. All of this combined with incredible music which is impacted by the weather and location results in a superior atmosphere which serves as a beautiful wrapping to this incredibly game.

Now for the negative. It is hard for me to find much here, but here are a few things:

  1. Although I mentioned I would not review the story as a whole, what story I have gone through so far is generic. A hero saving a princess. It is still good and more deep than I would have thought, but there isn’t a Bethesda story here. Not exactly overwhelmingly negative, but a slight hit to its perfection.
  2. Finally, I could complain about the leveling of monsters. It is a mechanic I hate in Elder Scrolls and while I have witnessed it (I think?) in Zelda, it is not too obvious, I still feel pretty powerful right now being 50–60 hours into the game, I am not getting destroyed by crabs and rats in starting areas like in Elder Scrolls. Overall an incredibly minor complaint which I barely notice.

Before I close this out let’s take one last look at the trailer:

At the end of the day, Zelda is almost everything I want in a video game.

It is fun, doesn’t take itself too seriously, has amazing environments, puzzles, wonderful music and art, incredible mechanics, gameplay, creative dialog and interaction, a smooth polished feeling and a better item/crafting/and stat system that while simple is more engaging than most games.

Perhaps the best line to sum this game up is wonderfully simple yet beautifully complex. This game is a masterpiece.

10/10

By Nathan P. Hoffman on March 25, 2017.
