The Moon in Poetry — Emily Dickinson

It has been a while since I have posted on my blog, so I figured I would come back on with one post per week, every-other will relate to astronomy/space in someway, the other will relate to my journey’s in code. I figured it might be fun to start with a poem about space.

The Moon was but a Chin of Gold
A Night or two ago — 
And now she turns Her perfect Face
Upon the World below —

Her Forehead is of Amplest Blonde — 
Her Cheek — a Beryl hewn — 
Her Eye unto the Summer Dew
The likest I have known —

Her Lips of Amber never part — 
But what must be the smile
Upon Her Friend she could confer
Were such Her Silver Will —

And what a privilege to be
But the remotest Star — 
For Certainty She take Her Way
Beside Your Palace Door —

Her Bonnet is the Firmament — 
The Universe — Her Shoe — 
The Stars — the Trinkets at Her Belt — 
Her Dimities — of Blue —

— Emily Dickinson

Stanza 1

The Moon was but a Chin of Gold
A Night or two ago — 
And now she turns Her perfect Face
Upon the World below —

It seems each stanza in this poem reflects a different aspect of the beauty or phase of the Moon, since we are personifying it.

Here, a chin of gold likely refers to the Moon in a gibbous phase (see: photo above) We know only a couple days later “she turns her face”

I have to admit, the image she creates here is most definitely a powerful one. A large Moon on the horizon not only looks gold, due to refraction, but appears to be gigantic due to human perception which misleads us into believing it to be huge.

Stanza 2

Her Forehead is of Amplest Blonde — 
Her Cheek — a Beryl hewn — 
Her Eye unto the Summer Dew
The likest I have known —

One of the coolest things about observing the Moon during daylight, is its contrast with the blue sky. This can be difficult to see with your own eyes due to the bright sky, but using a telescope, which enhances contrast, you can easily resolve a surface which is a foggy white, submerged in a sea of blue. What I find particularly interesting is that rather than use something like sapphire to exaggerate the color of blue, Emily uses the far more muted Beryl, likely referring to a cloudy-aquamarine color.

I think this perfectly describes the more muted tones of the sky mixing with the foggy white of the Moon’s apparent color during daylight. This is further supported by the Beryl “hewing” the Moon, as if it is cutting into.

Stanza 3

Her Lips of Amber never part — 
But what must be the smile
Upon Her Friend she could confer
Were such Her Silver Will —

Here we get a reference that seems to indicate a crescent shape (the smile). Some believe this proves the Moon is a crescent throughout the poem but I see it as a description of the Moon as a whole, not at one specific time. She is being personified afterall -she is alive.

We also get a reference to silver for the first time, again it seems like we are changing windows of time now perhaps the Moon is high above us at a future date? We also wonder why her lips of Amber never part? The Moon is only amber at the horizon, she seems to be telling us that there is an ensnared beauty to The Moon, a magical quality which is not lost simply because those lips part, or the Moon rises. (which is suggested by it being silver/gray)

Stanza 4

And what a privilege to be
But the remotest Star — 
For Certainty She take Her Way
Beside Your Palace Door —

Undoubtedly we are getting an image of heaven, the Moon reaching so high it passes by the palace doors. Religious imagery is often used in her poetry.

Stanza 5

Her Bonnet is the Firmament — 
The Universe — Her Shoe — 
The Stars — the Trinkets at Her Belt — 
Her Dimities — of Blue —

Firmament describes a solid dome of the sky, a classical belief held in astronomy. If she was situated against the firmament, by definition the firmament would wrap around her head like a bonnet.

The last point of interest is “Trinkets at Her Belt.” I may be reaching here, but for me I always associate “Belt” with Orion’s Belt, easily one of the most recognizable things in the night sky, much of its light coming from young blue giants that could rightfully be called trinkets or jewels. Perhaps when she follows with Dimities of Blue she is ending the poem with a statement that the Moon is so beautiful it is deserving of such a gown / dress / trinkets of the beautiful blue found in Orion?

I am definitely a large fan of Emily Dickinson, but as an astronomy fan, this poem really describes what it feels like to see the Moon in all its glory and phases. But I think what impresses me most, is that Emily obviously paid attention to the details of nature and the night-sky, so much so that it holds up even in our age of science if you take the more antiquated details (see: firmament) as artistic license instead of reality.
